You may have noticed that there often seems to be significant overlap between the definitions of different distortions. This is not something to worry about. If you find yourself having a hard time deciding which distortion is "correct," just go with your gut instinct, or pick one randomly. If you feel stuck between which of two distortions applies best, then you probably understand the generalized structure of how your cognition is distorted. What matters most is the final step, where you re-write your original cognition to be healthier and free of distortions.

Another valid worry is that negative emotions often have something important to tell us. After all, our emotions evolved for a reason. An easy standard to tell if a negative emotional state is healthy is whether or not it's free of character judgments and if it leaves you feeling encouraged to act on the cause of the negativity. For example, if you feel bad about procrastinating on your homework, and it leads you to resolve to start it when you get home, then the transient bad feeling was probably healthy. If the thought cascades into a mental diatribe about what a terrible person you are for procrastinating, leading you to want to bury thoughts of homework even further, then the emotion probably contains distortions, and it is worth writing an entry in the CBT journal unwinding those distortions.

Finally, remember that while CBT has improved the lives of many people, it is not for everybody. Do not view this app as a substitute for a mental health professional, and do not spurn treatment recommendations from such a professional in favor of this app.
