Emotional reasoning is when we take our emotions as bulletproof evidence of the underlying truth. "I feel like a loser, so I am one." However, if our emotions are suffering from underlying distortions, they will not be objective indicators of the truth. Emotional reasoning can affect not just our self-conception, but our relationship with others. For example, someone takes the fact that they feel angry at someone as automatic evidence of what a bad person they are, as opposed to examining the reasons why the feel so angry at that person. One surprising effect of emotional reasoning is procrastination. Oftentimes, we avoid a productive task because we tell ourselves that doing the task will feel so terrible. When we finally get around to it, we discover that it wasn't that bad, and feel embarrassed that we assigned so much negative emotion to it to justify the procrastination.
